Site Redesign: U.S. Department of the Interior

Problem

The Department of the Interior’s website contains many resources and detailed information, but it is not organized in an intuitive, clear, user-oriented way.

Solution

By improving the site’s navigation and design, we created a more enjoyable experience for our users and encouraged them to spend more time engaging with the website and its resources.

Details

Project Role: User Experience Designer and Researcher
Tools: Figma, Miro, Otter Transcription
Timeline: three weeks

01 Research

The first step in our redesign process was to conduct a heuristic analysis of the existing government website and annotate the primary site pages. These exercises helped us pinpoint the strengths and weaknesses of the site layout, navigation, design, and functionality. Using what our team knew about the DOI, we came up with two proto-personas to exemplify users who might visit this website and problems that they might encounter.

This helped us decide what primary functions of the site we needed to include in our user testing. While developing proto-personas is a helpful exercise, our site redesign needed to be based upon real user feedback about the site. We interviewed six participants, and asked them to complete a series of tasks to test the usability of the website, as well as to give us their overall impression of the current design.

Problem Statement

The Department of the Interior’s website contains many resources and detailed information, but it is not organized in an intuitive, clear, user-oriented way. This inhibits users from fully engaging with the DOI, its resources, and initiatives. How might we create a more enjoyable experience for users and ultimately increase quarterly site visits and the average amount of time people spend on the site?

02 ideate

Using our insights from user testing, we created a prioritization matrix to identify what features of the site were most important to the users.

User testing demonstrated that the site’s current organization of resources is not meeting the needs of the users; they had trouble locating specific information. With this pain point in mind, we conducted a card sorting activity to see how we might restructure the information architecture for our redesign.

In this phase, we also developed our style guide for use in our later prototypes.

03 prototype

Our prototypes and UI Style Direction were guided by the DOI’s mission statement as well as user testing feedback we received about the site’s visual appeal, navigation, and usability. Our low fidelity prototype is pictured below.

We created our mid-fidelity prototype by implementing our style guide and revisiting our user feedback to ensure our design choices prioritized the user.

Our biggest challenge was restructuring such a large amount of information on one website and ensuring new users understood what the DOI is and does. It was crucial for us that users could both visually and intuitively navigate the website.

04 testing & final iterations

We iterated our high-fidelity prototype based on user feedback from testing our mid-fidelity prototype. We also developed a mobile version of our site redesign to ensure users can find what they need from desktop and mobile devices.

05 conclusions

Our priority was to make as many paths to information as visible as possible without being overwhelming to our users.

We believe that our redesign will promote engagement and encourage users to spend more time exploring the site and the DOI.
